History

Nissan produced the Skyline GT-R between 1969 and 1974, a high-performance version of the Skyline coupe. It featured signature four round tail lights, ATTESA E-TS Pro all-wheel drive, and a twin-turbocharged 6-cylinder engine. However, it removed the HICAS four-wheel-steering system and replaced the straight-6 RB26DETT engine with the VR38DETT. The GT-R retained its predecessor's nickname, Godzilla.

Development

In 2000, Nissan decided to reintroduce the iconic GT-R as part of the Nissan Revival Plan, aiming to increase sales and public attention. CEO Carlos Ghosn aimed for the car to be a strong performance car that could compete with high-performance sports cars from brands like Porsche, Lamborghini, and Ferrari. The design aimed to adapt the signature feature of the GT-R, four round taillights. Designers from Japan, the United States, and Europe began creating new sketches for the GT-R, with 50 of them reaching Nissan's design director, Shiro Nakamura. Chief designer Hiroshi Hasegawa worked for over four years to complete the interior and exterior designs. Kazutoshi Mizuno, chief engineer of the Skyline GT-R R34, was appointed as the chief engineer in January 2004. Aerodynamics development began in early 2004 with a group of best engineers, including Yoshi Suzuka, who selected 12 sketches from various design studios.
